MAC (STANCE — CONTEXT)

Everything starts with stance.

If your stance is wrong,
everything else has to work harder.

Picture train tracks.
Your feet are on separate rails, not a tightrope.

A tightrope looks neat,
but it’s easy to knock you off balance.

Train tracks give you stability.

Front foot points forward.
Back foot turns out slightly.

Knees soft.
Weight balanced.

This lets you move, punch, and defend
without fighting your own body.

Hold it there.

Feel stable.

MAC (GUARD — CONTEXT)

Now the guard.

Your hands protect your head,
but they also protect your attention.

When the guard drops in boxing,
things slip through.

Life’s a bit like that too.
When your guard is down,
you miss what’s coming.

So this isn’t tension.
It’s readiness.

When I say hands up,
they stay up.

You’re not lifting and dropping them.
You’re setting them in place.

Think of resting your hands
on invisible shelves at cheek level.

We’ll wake the guard up.

Set of four.

Hands up. Three.
Hands up. Four.

One.

Four.

Last set.

One.
Two.
Three.
Four.

And stop. Stay in stance.

First punch.
The Jab.

This punch controls distance.
It tells the other person where they can and can’t stand.

Think of it like a wet towel.

Straight back.

It’s not about power.

Second punch.
The Cross.

This one travels from the ground up.

Power comes from turning the body,
not from swinging the arm.

Think of pushing a heavy door closed.
You don’t slam it.
You lean into it and let your body do the work.
